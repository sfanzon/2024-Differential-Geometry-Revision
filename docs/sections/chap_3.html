3.1 Preliminaries

Definition 1: Topology of \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

The Euclidean norm on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is denoted by \[ \| \mathbf{x}\| := \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 }\,, \quad \mathbf{x}= (x_1 , \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n \,. \] The Euclidean norm induces the distance \[ d(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) := \| \mathbf{x}- \mathbf{y}\| = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2 } \,. \]

In particular, we have:

  1. The pair \((\mathbb{R}^n,d)\) is a metric space.

  2. The topology induced by the metric \(d\) is called the Euclidean topology, denoted by \(\mathcal{T}\).

  3. A set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) is open if for all \(\mathbf{x}\in U\) there exists \(\varepsilon>0\) such that \(B_{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{x}) \subseteq U\), where \[ B_{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{x}) := \{ \mathbf{y}\in \mathbb{R}^n \, \colon \,\| \mathbf{x}- \mathbf{y}\| < \varepsilon\} \] is the open ball of radius \(\varepsilon>0\) and centered at \(\mathbf{x}\). In this case we write \(U \in \mathcal{T}\), with \(\mathcal{T}\) the Euclidean topology in \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

  4. A set \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) is closed if \(V^c := \mathbb{R}^n \smallsetminus U\) is open.

Definition 2: Subspace Topology
Given a subset \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) the subspace topology on \(A\) is the family of sets \[ \mathcal{T}_A := \{ U \subseteq A \, \colon \,\exists \,\, W \in \mathcal{T}\, \text{ s.t. } \, U = A \cap W \} \,. \] If \(U \in \mathcal{T}_A\), we say that \(U\) is open in \(A\).

Definition 3: Continuous Function
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) with \(U\) open. We say that \(f\) is continuous at \(\mathbf{x}\in U\) if \(\forall \, \varepsilon>0\), \(\exists \, \delta > 0\) such that \[ \| \mathbf{x}- \mathbf{y}\| < \delta \quad \implies \quad \| f(\mathbf{x}) - f (\mathbf{y}) \| < \varepsilon\,. \] \(f\) is continuous in \(U\) if it is continuous for all \(\mathbf{x}\in U\).

Theorem 4: Continuity: Topological definition
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m\), with \(U,V\) open. We have that \(f\) is continuous if and only if \(f^{-1}(A)\) is open in \(U\), for all \(A\) open in \(V\).

Definition 5: Homeomorphism

Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m\) with \(U,V\) open. We say that \(f\) is a homeomorphism if:

  1. \(f\) is continuous;
  2. There exists continuous inverse \(f^{-1} \colon V \to U\).

Definition 6: Differentiable Function
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) with \(U\) open. We say that \(f\) is differentiable at \(\mathbf{x}\in U\) if there exists a linear map \(d_{\mathbf{x}} f \colon \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) such that \[ d_{\mathbf{x}}f (\mathbf{h}) = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0} \ \frac{ f(\mathbf{x}+ \varepsilon\mathbf{h} ) - f(\mathbf{x}) }{ \varepsilon} \,, \] for all \(\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^n\), where the limit is taken in \(\mathbb{R}^m\). The linear map \(d_{\mathbf{x}} f\) is called the differential of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{x}\).

Definition 7: Partial Derivative
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\), \(U\) open, \(f\) differentiable. The partial derivative of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{x}\in U\) in direction \(\mathbf{e}_i\) is given by \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{x}) := d_{\mathbf{x}} f (\mathbf{e}_i) = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0} \frac{ f( \mathbf{x}+ \varepsilon\mathbf{e}_i ) - f(\mathbf{x}) }{ \varepsilon} \,. \]

Definition 8: Jacobian Matrix
Let \(f \colon U \subset \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) be differentiable. The Jacobian of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{x}\) is the \(m \times n\) matrix of partial derivatives: \[ Jf(\mathbf{x}):= \left( \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(\mathbf{x}) \right)_{i,j} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \,. \] If \(m=n\) then \(Jf \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is a square matrix and we can compute its determinant, denoted by \[ \det (Jf) \,. \]

Proposition 9: Matrix representation of \(d_{\mathbf{x}} f\)
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) be differentiable. The matrix of the linear map \(d_{\mathbf{x}} f \colon \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) with respect to the standard basis is given by the Jacobian matrix \(Jf(\mathbf{x})\).

Definition 10: Smooth Function
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) with \(U\) open. We say that \(f\) is smooth if the derivatives \[ \frac{\partial^{|\alpha|} f}{d\mathbf{x}^\alpha} := \frac{\partial^{\alpha_1}}{ \partial x_1^{\alpha_1}} \cdots \frac{\partial^{\alpha_n}}{ \partial x_n^{\alpha_n}} \, f \] exist for each multi-index \(\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^n\). Note that in this case all the derivatives of \(f\) are automatically continuous.

Notation 11: Gradient and partial derivatives
Let \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) be smooth. We denote the partial derivatives by \[ \begin{aligned} \partial_{x_i} f & = f_{x_i} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \\ \partial_{x_i x_j} f & = f_{x_i x_j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \\ \partial_{x_i x_j x_k} f & = f_{x_i x_j x_k} = \frac{\partial^3 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j \partial x_k} \end{aligned} \]

For \(f \colon U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) smooth we denote the gradient by \[ \nabla f (\mathbf{x}) = \left( f_{x_1}(\mathbf{x}) , \ldots , f_{x_n}(\mathbf{x}) \right) \,. \] Note that \(\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\) coincides with \(Jf(\mathbf{x})\).

Definition 12: Diffeomorphism

Let \(f \colon U \to V\), with \(U,V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) open. We say that \(f\) is a diffeomorphism between \(U\) and \(V\) if:

  1. \(f\) is smooth,
  2. There exists the inverse \(f^{-1} \colon V \to U\),
  3. \(f^{-1}\) is smooth.
Definition 13: Local diffeomorphism

A function \(f \colon \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is a local diffeomorphism at \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n\) if:

  1. There exists an open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in U\),
  2. There exists an open set \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(f(\mathbf{x}_0) \in V\),
  3. \(f \colon U \to V\) is a diffeomorphism.

Theorem 14
If \(f \colon U \to V\) is a diffeomorphism, then \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism at each \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in U\).

Theorem 15
Let \(f \colon U \to \mathbb{R}^n\) with \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) open. Suppose \(f\) is a local diffeomorhism at \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in U\). Then \[ \det Jf (\mathbf{x}_0) \neq 0 \,. \]

Theorem 16: Inverse Function Theorem

Let \(f \colon U \to \mathbb{R}^n\) with \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) open, \(f\) smooth. Assume \[ \det J f(\mathbf{x}_0) \neq 0 \,, \] for some \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in U\). Then:

  1. There exists an open set \(U_0 \subseteq U\) such that \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in U_0\),
  2. There exists an open set \(V\) such that \(f(\mathbf{x}_0) \in V\),
  3. \(f \colon U_0 \to V\) is a diffeomorphism.
Example 17

Question. Consider the function \(f \colon \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2\) \[ f(x,y) = (e^x \cos(y), e^x \sin(y)) \,. \]

  1. Prove that \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism at each point.

  2. Prove that \(f\) is not a diffeomorphism.

Solution.

  1. We compute \[\begin{align*} & J f (x,y) = e^x \left( \begin{array}{cc} \cos(y) & \sin(y) \\ -\sin(y) & \cos(y) \end{array} \right) \\ & \det Jf(x,y) = e^{2x} \neq 0 \end{align*}\] By the Inverse Function Theorem, \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism at each point \((x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2\).

  2. \(f\) is not injective, since \[ f(x,y) = f(x, y + 2n\pi) \,, \quad \forall\, (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 , \, n \in \mathbb{N}\,. \] Therefore \(f\) is not invertible from \(\mathbb{R}^2\) into \(\mathbb{R}^2\). This implies \(f\) cannot be a diffeomorphism of \(\mathbb{R}^2\) into \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

3.2 Regular surfaces

Definition 18: Surface
Let \(\mathcal{S}\subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\) be a connected set. We say that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a surface if for every point \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\) there exist

  1. An open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\),
  2. A smooth map \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \subseteq \mathcal{S}\) such that
    • \(\mathbf{p}\in {\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\),
    • \({\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\) is open in \(\mathcal{S}\)
    • \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is a homeomorphism between \(U\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\)

The homeomorphism \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is called a surface chart at \(\mathbf{p}\).

Definition 19: Atlas of a surface
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a surface. Assume given a collection of charts \[ \mathcal{A} = \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_i\}_{i \in I} \,, \qquad {\pmb{\sigma}}_i \colon U_i \to {\pmb{\sigma}}(U_i) \subseteq \mathcal{S}\,. \] The family \(\mathcal{A}\) is an atlas of \(\mathcal{S}\) if \[ \mathcal{S}= \bigcup_{i \in I} {\pmb{\sigma}}_i(U_i) \,. \]

Definition 20: Regular Chart
Let \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) be open. A map \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}= {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) \colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3 \] is called a regular chart if the partial derivatives \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u(u,v) = \frac{d{\pmb{\sigma}}}{du}(u,v) \,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v(u,v) = \frac{d{\pmb{\sigma}}}{dv}(u,v) \] are linearly independent vectors of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) for all \((u,v) \in U\).

Definition 21: Regular surface

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a surface. We say that:

  • \(\mathcal{A}\) is a regular atlas if any \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) in \(\mathcal{A}\) is regular.
  • \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface if it admits a regular atlas.
Theorem 22

Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) with \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) open. They are equivalent

  1. \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is a regular chart.
  2. \(d_{\mathbf{x}} {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is injective for all \(\mathbf{x}\in U\).
  3. The Jacobian matrix \(J {\pmb{\sigma}}\) has rank \(2\) for all \((u,v) \in U\).
  4. \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \neq 0\) for all \((u,v) \in U\).

Example 23: 2D Plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)
Question. Let \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^3\), with \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) orthonormal. The plane \[ {\pmb{\pi}}= \{ \mathbf{a} + u \mathbf{p}+ v \mathbf{q} \, \colon \,u,v \in \mathbb{R}\} \] is a surface with atlas \(\mathcal{A} = \{{\pmb{\sigma}}\}\), where \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon \mathbb{R}^2 \to {\pmb{\pi}}\,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v):= \mathbf{a} + u \mathbf{p}+ v \mathbf{q} \,. \] Prove that \({\pmb{\pi}}\) is a regular surface.

Solution. We have \({{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}= \mathbf{p}, {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = \mathbf{q}\). Since \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) are orthonormal, we conclude that \({{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\) and \({{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}\) are linearly independent and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular. \({\pmb{\pi}}\) is a regular surface because \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is a regular chart.

Example 24: Unit cylinder
Question. Consider the infinite unit cylinder \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,x^2 + y^2 = 1 \} \,. \] \(\mathcal{S}\) is a surface with atlas \(\mathcal{A} = \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_1,{\pmb{\sigma}}_2\}\), where \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}& \colon \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, & {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) & = (\cos(u),\sin(u),v)\,, \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_1 & := {\pmb{\sigma}}|_{U_1} \,, & {\pmb{\sigma}}_2 & := {\pmb{\sigma}}|_{U_2} \,, \\ U_1 & := \left( 0,\frac{ 3 \pi}{2} \right) \times \mathbb{R}\,, & U_2 & := \left( \pi,\frac{ 5 \pi}{2} \right) \times \mathbb{R}\,. \end{align*}\] Prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

Solution. The map \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular because \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_u & = (-\sin(u),\cos(u),0)\\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v & = (0,0,1) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v & = (\cos (u), \sin(u), 0) \\ \left\| {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \right\| & = 1 \neq 0 \,. \end{align*}\] Therefore \({\pmb{\sigma}}_1\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_2\) are regular charts, being restrictions of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\). Thus, \(\mathcal{A}\) is a regular atlas, making \(\mathcal{S}\) a regular surface.

Example 25: Graph of a function
Question. Let \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) be open and \(f \colon U \to \mathbb{R}\) be smooth. The graph of \(f\) is the set \[ \Gamma_f := \{ (u,v,f(u,v)) \, \colon \,(u,v) \in U \} \,. \] \(\Gamma_f\) is surface with atlas given by \(\mathcal{A} = \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}\}\), where \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \Gamma_f \,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v):=(u,v,f(u,v)) \,. \] Prove that \(\Gamma_f\) is a regular surface.

Solution. The Jacobian matrix of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ J{\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ f_u & f_v \\ \end{array} \right) \,. \] The first minor of \(J{\pmb{\sigma}}\) is the identity matrix, which has determinant \(1\), and is hence invertible. Therefore \(J{\pmb{\sigma}}\) has rank 2, showing that \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) are linearly independent. Hence \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular. This implies \(\mathcal{A}\) is a regular atlas, and \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

Definition 26: Spherical coordinates
A point \(\mathbf{p}= (x,y,z) \neq {\pmb{0}}\) is represented in spherical coordinates by \[\begin{align*} x & = \rho \cos(\theta) \cos (\varphi) \\ y & = \rho \sin(\theta) \cos (\varphi) \\ z & = \rho \sin (\varphi) \end{align*}\] where \[ \rho:=\sqrt{ x^2 + y^2 + z^2 } \,, \quad \theta \in [-\pi,\pi] \,, \quad \varphi\in \left[ -\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2} \right] \,. \]

Example 27: Unit sphere in spherical coordinates
Question. Consider the unit sphere in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) \[ \mathbb{S}^2 = \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1 \} \,. \] Define the set \[ U = \left\{ (\theta,\varphi) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \, \colon \,\theta \in ( -\pi,\pi), \, \varphi\in \left( -\frac{\pi}{2} , \frac{\pi}{2} \right) \right\} \,, \] and the chart \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}( \theta , \varphi) := ( \cos(\theta) \cos(\varphi) , \sin(\theta) \cos(\varphi) , \sin (\varphi) ) \,. \] Prove that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.

Solution. We compute \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} & = (-\sin(\theta) \cos(\varphi), \cos(\theta) \cos(\varphi), 0 ) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\varphi} & = ( - \cos(\theta) \sin(\varphi), -\sin(\theta) \sin(\varphi), \cos(\varphi) ) \,. \end{align*}\] Since \((\theta,\varphi)\in U\), we have \(\varphi\in ( -\pi/2, \pi/2 )\). Therefore, the last component \(\cos(\varphi)\) of \({\pmb{\sigma}}_\varphi\) is non-zero. As the last component of \({\pmb{\sigma}}_\theta\) is \(0\), we conclude that \({\pmb{\sigma}}_\theta\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_{\varphi}\) are linearly independent for all \((\theta,\varphi) \in U\). Therefore \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular. Alternatively, compute \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\varphi} & = ( \cos(\theta) \cos^2(\varphi), \sin(\theta) \cos^2(\varphi), \cos(\varphi)\sin(\varphi) ) \\ \left\| {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\varphi} \right\| & = |\cos (\varphi)| = \cos (\varphi) \neq 0 \,, \end{align*}\] since \(\varphi\in ( -\pi/2, \pi/2 )\), showing that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.

Example 28
Question. Prove that the following chart is not regular \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (u,v^2,v^3) \,. \]

Solution. We have \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_u & = (1,0,0) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v & = (0,2v,3v^2) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v(u,0) & = (0,0,0) \,, \end{align*}\] showing that \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) are linearly dependent along the line \[ L = \{ (u,0) \, \colon \,u \in \mathbb{R}\} \,. \] Hence \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is not a regular chart.

3.3 Reparametrizations

Definition 29: Reparametrization
Suppose that \(U, \widetilde{U} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) are open sets and \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, \] are surface charts. We say that \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) is a reparametrization of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) if there exists a diffeomorphism \(\Phi \colon \widetilde{U} \to U\) such that \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = {\pmb{\sigma}}\circ \Phi \,. \] We call \(\Phi\) a reparametrization map.

Theorem 30
Let \(U, \widetilde{U} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) be open and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be regular. Suppose given a diffeomorphism \(\Phi \colon \widetilde{U} \to U\). The reparametrization \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \mathbb{R}^3\,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = {\pmb{\sigma}}\circ \Phi \] is a regular chart, and it holds \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{\tilde u} \times \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{\tilde v} =\det J \Phi \, \left( {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\times {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}\right) \,. \]

Definition 31: Transition map
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \mathcal{S} \] be regular charts. Suppose the images of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) overlap, that is, \[ I := {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \cap \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} (\widetilde{U}) \neq \emptyset \,. \] The set \(I\) is open in \(\mathcal{S}\), being intersection of open sets. Define the sets \[ V := {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(I) \subseteq U \,, \quad \widetilde{V} := \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} (I) \subseteq \widetilde{U} \,. \] Note that \(V\) and \(\widetilde{V}\) are open, by continuity of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\). Since \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) are homeomorphisms, by construction \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(V) = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} (\widetilde{V} ) = I \,. \] Therefore, they are well defined the restrictions \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}|_{V} \colon V \to I \,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} |_{\widetilde{V}} \colon \widetilde{V} \to I \,. \] The maps \({\pmb{\sigma}}|_{V}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} |_{\widetilde{V}}\) are homeomorphisms, being restrictions of homeomorphisms. The composition homeomorphism \[ \Phi \colon \widetilde{V} \to V \,, \quad \Phi := {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1} \circ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \] is called a transition map from \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) to \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\).

Theorem 32
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface. The transition maps between regular charts are diffeomorphisms.

Theorem 33
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \mathcal{S} \] be regular charts. Suppose the images of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) overlap, that is, \[ I := {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \cap \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} (\widetilde{U}) \neq \emptyset \,. \] Define the open sets \[ V := {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(I) \subseteq U \,, \quad \widetilde{V} := \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1}(I) \subseteq \widetilde{U} \,, \] and the transition map \[ \Phi \colon \widetilde{V} \to V \,, \quad \Phi := {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}\circ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\,. \] Then \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) are reparametrization of each other, with reparametrization map given by \(\Phi\), that is, \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}= {\pmb{\sigma}}\circ \Phi \,, \qquad {\pmb{\sigma}}= \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\circ \Phi^{-1} \,. \]

3.4 Functions between surfaces

Definition 34: Smooth functions between surfaces

Let \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2\) a map.

  1. \(f\) is smooth at \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\), if \(\exists\) charts \({\pmb{\sigma}}_i \colon U_i \to \mathcal{S}_i\) for \(i=1,2\) such that \[ \mathbf{p}\in {\pmb{\sigma}}_1(U_1)\,, \quad f(\mathbf{p}) \in {\pmb{\sigma}}_2(U_2) \] and \[ ({\pmb{\sigma}}_2^{-1} \circ f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}_1 ) \colon U_1 \to U_2 \] is smooth.

  2. \(f\) is smooth, if it is smooth for each \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\).

Theorem 35
If \(f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2\) and \(g \colon \mathcal{S}_2 \to \mathcal{S}_3\) are smooth maps between surfaces, then the composition \[ (g \circ f) \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_3 \] is smooth.

Theorem 36
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface, and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) a regular chart. The inverse \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1} \colon {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \to U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \,, \] is a differentiable function.

Theorem 37
Let \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) be regular surfaces. Make the following assumptions:

  1. \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\) is an open set such that \[ \mathcal{S}_1 \subseteq V \,, \]
  2. \(f \colon V \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is a differentiable function such that
    \[ f(\mathcal{S}_1) \subseteq \mathcal{S}_2 \,. \]

Then the restriction \[ f |_{\mathcal{S}_1} \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2 \] is a differentiable map between surfaces.

Definition 38: Diffeomorphism of surfaces

Let \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) be regular surfaces.

  1. A map \(f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2\) is a diffeomorphism, if \(f\) is smooth and admits smooth inverse.

  2. We say that \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) are diffeomorphic, if there exists \(f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2\) diffeomorphism.

Theorem 39
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces, \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) diffeomorphism. If \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) is a regular chart for \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(\mathbf{p}\), then \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} := f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\,, \] is a regular chart for \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) at \(f(\mathbf{p})\).

Definition 40: Local diffeomorphism

Let \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) be regular surfaces, and \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2 \,, \] be a differentiable map.

  1. \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism at \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\) if:

    • There exists An open set \(V \subseteq \mathcal{S}_1\) with \(\mathbf{p}\in V\);
    • \(f(V) \subseteq \mathcal{S}_2\) is open;
    • \(f \colon V \to f(V)\) is a diffeomorphism between surfaces.

  2. \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism in \(\mathcal{S}_1\), if it is a local diffeomorphism at each \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\).

  3. \(\mathcal{S}_1\) is locally diffeomorphic to \(\mathcal{S}_2\), if for each \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\) there exists \(f\) local diffeomorphism at \(\mathbf{p}\).

3.5 Tangent plane

Definition 41: Tangent vectors and tangent plane

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a surface and \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\).

  1. We say that \(\mathbf{v}\in \mathbb{R}^3\) is a tangent vector to \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(\mathbf{p}\), if there exists a smooth curve \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, \] such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(-\varepsilon, \varepsilon) \subseteq \mathcal{S}\,, \quad {\pmb{\gamma}}(0) = \mathbf{p}\,, \quad \mathbf{v}= \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(0) \,, \] where \(\varepsilon>0\).

  2. The tangent plane of \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(\mathbf{p}\) is the set \[ T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}:= \{ \mathbf{v}\in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,\mathbf{v}\, \mbox{ tangent vector of } \, \mathcal{S}\, \mbox{ at } \, \mathbf{p}\} \,. \]

Theorem 42: Curves with values on surfaces
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface, \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\), and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be a chart at \(\mathbf{p}\). Denote \[ (u_0,v_0) = {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(\mathbf{p}) \,. \] Suppose \({\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is a smooth curve such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) \in \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \forall \, t \in (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \,. \] Then, there exist smooth functions \[ u , v \colon (\varepsilon_0,\varepsilon_0) \to \mathbb{R}\,,\quad u(0)=u_0 \,, \quad v(0) = v_0 \,, \] such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}( u(t), v(t) ) \,, \quad \forall \, t \in (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \,. \]

Theorem 43: Characterization of Tangent Plane
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\). Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be a chart at \(\mathbf{p}\). Denote \[ (u_0,v_0) = {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(\mathbf{p}) \,. \] The tangent plane satisfies \[ T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}= \operatorname{span} \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u , {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \} := \{ \lambda {\pmb{\sigma}}_u + \mu {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \, \colon \,\lambda,\mu \in \mathbb{R}\} \,, \] where \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) are evaluated at \((u_0,v_0)\).

Theorem 44: Equation of tangent plane

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\). Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be a regular chart at \(\mathbf{p}\). Set \[ (u_0,v_0) := {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(\mathbf{p}) \,, \quad \mathbf{n}:= {\pmb{\sigma}}_u (u_0,v_0) \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v (u_0,v_0) \,. \] They hold:

  1. The equation of the tangent plane \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) is given by \[ \mathbf{x}\cdot \mathbf{n}= 0 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{x}\in \mathbb{R}^3 \,. \]

  2. The equation of the affine tangent plane \(\mathbf{p}+ T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) is given by \[ (\mathbf{x}- \mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{n}= 0 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{x}\in \mathbb{R}^3 \,. \]

Example 45

Question. Consider the surface \(\mathcal{S}\) defined by the chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) := \left( \sqrt{1-v} \cos(u) , \sqrt{1-v} \sin(u), v \right) \,, \] for \(u \in (0,2\pi)\), \(v < 1\).

  1. Prove that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) parametrizes a paraboloid.

  2. Prove that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.

  3. Compute the vector \(\mathbf{n}= {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\times {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}\)

  4. Consider the point \[ \mathbf{p}= \left( \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}, \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}, 0 \right) \,. \] Give a basis for \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) at the point

  5. Compute the cartesian equation of \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\). Give your answer in the form \[ ax + by + cz = d\,, \] for suitable \(a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{R}\).

  1.   2. Proof that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular: \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_u & = \left( - \sqrt{1-v} \sin(u) , \sqrt{1-v} \cos(u), 0 \right) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v & = \left( - \frac{1}{2} (1-v)^{-1/2} \cos(u) , -\frac{1}{2} (1-v)^{-1/2} \sin(u), 1 \right) \end{align*}\] The last component of \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) is \(0\), and the last component of \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) is \(1\), thus \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) are linearly independent. Hence \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.

  3.   4. Notice that \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\left( \frac{\pi}{4}, 0 \right) = \mathbf{p}\,. \] A basis for \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) is given by the vectors \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \left( \frac{\pi}{4}, 0 \right) & = \left( - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} , \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} , 0 \right)\,,\\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \left( \frac{\pi}{4}, 0 \right) & = \left( - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} , - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} , 1 \right) \,. \end{align*}\]

3.6 Unit normal and orientability

Definition 46: Standard unit normal of a chart
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) a regular chart. The standard unit normal of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is the smooth function \[ \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, \quad \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = \frac{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v }{ \left\| {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \right\| } \,. \]

Example 47: Standard unit normal of the plane
Let \(\mathbf{a} , \mathbf{p},\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^3\), with \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) linearly independent. Consider the plane charted by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v):= \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{p}u + \mathbf{q} v \,, \quad \forall \, (u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \,. \] Compute the standard unit normal to \({\pmb{\sigma}}\).

Solution. We have \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = \mathbf{p}\,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = \mathbf{q} \,, \quad \] and therefore \[ \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = \frac{ \mathbf{p}\times \mathbf{q} }{ \left\| \mathbf{p}\times \mathbf{q} \right\| } \,. \]

Definition 48: Unit normal of a surface
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface. A standard unit normal to \(\mathcal{S}\) is a smooth function \(\mathbf{N}\colon \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \[ \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{p}) \perp T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \| \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{p}) \| = 1 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\, . \]

Definition 49: Orientable surface
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface. We say that \(\mathcal{S}\) is orientable if there exists a standard unit normal \(\mathbf{N}\colon \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R}^3\) and an atlas \(\mathcal{A}\) such that \[ \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}= \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \,, \qquad \forall \, {\pmb{\sigma}}\in \mathcal{A} \,. \]

Example 50

Question. Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the surface described by the chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (e^u, u+v, v ) \,, \quad u,v \in \mathbb{R}\,. \]

  1. Prove that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.
  2. Compute the standard unit normal to \({\pmb{\sigma}}\).

Solution.

  1. Compute the following quantities: \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_{u} & =\left(e^{u}, 1,0\right) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_{v} & =(0,1,1) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_{u} \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_{v} & = \left|\begin{array}{ccc} \mathbf{i}& \mathbf{j}& \mathbf{k}\\ e^{u} & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\right| \\ & = \left(1,-e^{u}, e^{u}\right) \end{align*}\] Since \[ \left\| {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \right\| = \sqrt{1+2 e^{2 u}} \geq 1\,, \] we see that \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \neq {\pmb{0}}\), showing that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular.

  2. The standard unit normal to \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathbf{N}= \frac{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v }{ \left\| {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \right\| } = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+2 e^{2 u}}} \, \left(1,-e^{u}, e^{u}\right) \,. \]

3.7 Differential of smooth functions

Definition 51: Differential of smooth function
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) a smooth map. The differential \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\) of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{p}\) is defined as the map \[ d_{\mathbf{p}}f \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to T_{f(\mathbf{p})} \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \quad d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{v}) := (f \circ {\pmb{\gamma}})'(0) \,, \] where \({\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \to \mathcal{S}\) is any smooth curve such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(0) = \mathbf{p}\,, \qquad \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(0) = \mathbf{v}\,. \]

Example 52: Computing \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\) using the definition

Question. Consider the portion of the plane \(\{z=0\}\), \[ \mathcal{S}= \left\{ (x,y,0) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x \in (0,2\pi)\,, \, y \in \mathbb{R}\right\} \,, \] and the unit cylinder \[ \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} = \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x^2 + y^2 = 1 \} \,. \] Define the map \[ f \colon S \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \quad f(x,y,0) := (\cos x , \sin x, y) \,. \]

  1. Compute \(T_{\mathbf{p}}\mathcal{S}\).

  2. Prove that the differential \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\) at \[ \mathbf{p}= (u_0,v_0,0) \,, \quad \mathbf{v}= (\lambda,\mu,0) \,, \] is given by \[ d_{\mathbf{p}} f (\mathbf{v}) = (- \lambda \sin (u_0), \lambda \cos (u_0), \mu) \,. \]

Solution.

  1. A chart for \(\mathcal{S}\) is given by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (u,v,0) \,. \] Therefore \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = (1,0,0) \,, \qquad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = (0,1,0) \,, \] from which \[\begin{align*} T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}& = \operatorname{span} \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u,{\pmb{\sigma}}_v \} \\ & = \operatorname{span} \{ (1,0,0), (0,1,0) \} \\ & = \{ (\lambda,\mu,0) \, \colon \, \lambda,\mu \in \mathbb{R}\} \,. \end{align*}\]

  2. From the answer to Point 1, we see that \[ \mathbf{v}= (\lambda,\mu,0) \in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\,. \] Define the curve \({\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) \to \mathcal{S}\) by setting \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) := {\pmb{\sigma}}(u_0 + t \lambda, v_0 + t \mu ) = (u_0 + t \lambda, v_0 + t \mu, 0)\,. \] Clearly, \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(0) = \mathbf{p}\,, \quad \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(0) = \mathbf{v}= (\lambda,\mu,0)\,. \] We have \[\begin{align*} (f \circ {\pmb{\gamma}}) (t) & = f(u_0 + t \lambda, v_0 + t \mu,0) \\ & = ( \cos(u_0 + t \lambda), \sin(u_0 + t \lambda),v_0 + t \mu ) \,,\\ (f \circ {\pmb{\gamma}})' (t) & = (- \lambda \sin (u_0 + t\lambda), \lambda \cos (u_0 + t \lambda), \mu) \,. \end{align*}\] Therefore, the differential is given by \[ d_{\mathbf{p}} f (\mathbf{v}) = (f \circ {\pmb{\gamma}})' (0) = (- \lambda \sin (u_0), \lambda \cos (u_0), \mu) \,. \]

Theorem 53

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) a smooth map. Denote the differential of \(f\) by \[ d_{\mathbf{p}}f \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to T_{f(\mathbf{p})} \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,. \] We have:

  1. \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{v})\) depends only on \(f, \mathbf{p},\mathbf{v}\) (and not on \({\pmb{\gamma}}\)).

  2. \(d_{\mathbf{p}} f\) is linear, that is, \[ d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\lambda \mathbf{v}+ \mu \mathbf{w}) = \lambda d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{v}) + \mu d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{w}) \,, \] for all \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) and \(\lambda,\mu \in \mathbb{R}\).

  3. Let \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \] be regular charts at \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(f(\mathbf{p})\), respectively. Denote by \[ (u,v) \mapsto ( \alpha(u,v), \beta(u,v) ) \] the components of the smooth map \[ \Psi := \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} \circ f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \widetilde{U} \,. \] In particular it holds \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} ( \alpha(u,v) , \beta(u,v) ) = f({\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v)) \,, \quad \forall \, (u,v) \in U \,. \] The matrix of the linear map \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\) with respect to the basis \[ \{ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u , {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \} \,\, \mbox{ on } \,\, T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\,, \quad \{ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{\tilde{u}} , \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{\tilde{v}} \} \,\, \mbox{ on } \,\, T_{f(\mathbf{p})} \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \] is given by the Jacobian of the map \(\Psi\), that is, \[ d_{\mathbf{p}} f = J\Psi = \left( \begin{array}{cc} \alpha_u & \alpha_v \\ \beta_u & \beta_v \\ \end{array} \right) \,. \]

Example 54: Computing the matrix of \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\)
Question. Consider the unit cylinder, charted by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (\cos u, \sin u, v) \,, \quad (u,v) \in U := (0,2\pi) \times \mathbb{R}\,. \] Consider the plane \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) charted by \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}(u,v) = (u,v,0) \,, \quad (u,v) \in \widetilde{U}= \mathbb{R}^2 \,. \] Define the map \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \quad f(x,y,z) = (y,xz,0) \,. \] Compute the matrix of \(d_{\mathbf{p}} f\) with respect to these charts.

Solution. We need to compute the map \[ \Psi \colon U \to \widetilde{U} \,, \quad \Psi := \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} \circ f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\,. \] Clearly, we have \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} (u,v,0) =(u,v) \,. \] Therefore \[\begin{align*} \Psi(u,v) & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} \left( f({\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v)) \right) \\ & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} \left( f(\cos u, \sin u, v ) \right) \\ & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}^{-1} \left( \sin (u), \cos (u) v, 0 \right) \\ & = \left( \sin (u), \cos (u) v \right) \,. \end{align*}\] The components of \(\Psi\) are \[ \alpha(u,v) = \sin(u) \,, \qquad \beta (u,v) = \cos (u) v \,. \] Therefore \[\begin{align*} \alpha_u & = \cos (u) \,, & \alpha_v & = 0 \\ \beta_u & = -\sin (u) v \,, & \beta_v & = \cos(u) \end{align*}\] The matrix of \(d_{\mathbf{p}}f\) is hence \[ d_{\mathbf{p}}f = J\Psi = \left( \begin{array}{cc} \cos (u) & 0 \\ -\sin (u) v & \cos(u) \\ \end{array} \right) \,. \]

Theorem 55

Let \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and \(\mathcal{S}_2\) be regular surfaces. Suppose that \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}_1 \to \mathcal{S}_2 \] is smooth, and let \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_1\). They are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is a local diffeomorphism at \(\mathbf{p}\).
  2. The differential \(d_{\mathbf{p}} f \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}_1 \to T_{f(\mathbf{p})} \mathcal{S}_2\) is invertible at \(\mathbf{p}\).

3.8 Level surfaces

Definition 56: Level surface
Let \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\) be an open set and \(f \colon V \to \mathbb{R}\) be smooth. The level surface associated with \(f\) is the set \[ \mathcal{S}_f := f^{-1}(0) = \{ (x,y,z) \in V \, \colon \,f(x,y,z) = 0 \} \,. \]

Theorem 57
Let \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\) be an open set and \(f \colon V \to \mathbb{R}\) be smooth. Consider the level surface \[ \mathcal{S}_f = \{ (x,y,z) \in V \, \colon \,f(x,y,z) = 0 \} \,. \] Suppose that \[ \nabla f (x,y,z) \neq 0 \,, \quad \forall \, (x,y,z) \in V \,. \] Then \(\mathcal{S}_f\) is a regular surface.

Example 58: Circular cone
Question. Consider the circular cone \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,x^2 + y^2 = z^2 \,, \, z>0 \} \,. \] Prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

Solution. Define the open set \[ V = \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,z > 0 \} \,, \] and the function \(f \colon V \to \mathbb{R}\) by \[ f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 - z^2 \,. \] We have \[ \nabla f (x,y,z) = ( 2x, 2y, -2z ) \neq 0 \,, \quad \forall \, (x,y,z) \in V \,. \] Since \[ \mathcal{S}= \mathcal{S}_f \,, \] we conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

Theorem 59: Tangent plane of level surfaces

Let \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3\) be an open set and \(f \colon V \to \mathbb{R}\) be smooth. Suppose that \[ \nabla f (x,y,z) \neq 0 \,, \quad \forall \, (x,y,z) \in V \,. \] Let \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}_f\). We have \[ \nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \perp T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}_f \,. \] In particular, they hold:

  1. The cartesian equation of \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}_f\) is given by \[ \nabla f (\mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{x}= 0 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{x}\in \mathbb{R}^3\,, \]

  2. The cartesian equation for \(\mathbf{p}+ T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}_f\) is given by \[ \nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \cdot (\mathbf{x}- \mathbf{p}) = 0 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{x}\in \mathbb{R}^3\,. \]

Example 60: Unit cylinder

Question. Consider the unit cylinder \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \,x^2 + y^2 = 1 \} \,. \]

  1. Prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.
  2. Let \[ \mathbf{p}= \left( \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},5 \right) \in \mathcal{S} \] Show that \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) has cartesian equation \[ x + y = 0 \,. \]

Solution.

  1. Define the open set \[ V = \mathbb{R}^3 \smallsetminus \{ (0,0,z) \, \colon \,z \in \mathbb{R}\} \,. \] Note that \(V\) is obtained by removing the \(z\)-axis from \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Also define the function \(f \colon \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}\) by \[ f(x,y,z) := x^2 + y^2 -1 \,. \] We have \[ \nabla f (x,y,z) = ( 2x, 2y, 0 ) \neq 0 \,, \quad \forall \, (x,y,z) \in V \,. \] Since \[ \mathcal{S}= \mathcal{S}_f \,, \] we conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

  2. Using the expression for \(\nabla f\) found in Point 1, we get \[ \nabla f (\mathbf{p}) = \nabla f \left( \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},5 \right) = (\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2},0) \,. \] The equation for \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) is \[ \nabla f (\mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{x}= 0 \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{x}\in \mathbb{R}^3 \,. \] Therefore, we find \[\begin{align*} \nabla f (\mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{x}= 0 \,\, & \iff \,\, (\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2},0) \cdot (x,y,z) = 0 \\ \,\, & \iff \,\, x + y = 0 \,. \end{align*}\]

3.9 Ruled surfaces

Definition 61: Ruled surface

A ruled surface is a surface with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) + v \mathbf{a}(u) \,, \] where

  1. \({\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (a,b) \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is a smooth curve,
  2. \(\mathbf{a} \colon (a,b) \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is a smooth curve,
  3. \(\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t)\) and \(\mathbf{a}(t)\) are linearly independent for all \(t \in (a,b)\).

We say that:

  1. \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) is the base curve.
  2. The lines \(v \mapsto v \mathbf{a}(u)\) are the rulings.

Theorem 62
A ruled surface \(\mathcal{S}\) is regular if \(v\) is sufficiently small.

Example 63: Unit Cylinder is ruled surface
Question. Prove that the unit cylinder \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x^2 + y^2 = 1 \} \,. \] is a ruled surface.

Solution. We know that the unit cylinder is charted by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (\cos(u), \cos(u),v ) \,, \quad (u,v) \in (0,2\pi) \times \mathbb{R}\,. \] We can rewrite \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) as \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) + v \mathbf{a}(u) \,, \] with \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(u):= (\cos(u), \cos(u),0 ) \,, \quad \mathbf{a} = (0,0,1) \,. \] Note that the vectors \[ \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}= (-\sin(u), \cos(u), 0 ) \,, \quad \mathbf{a} = (0,0,1) \] are orthogonal, and hence linearly independent. Therefore \(\mathcal{S}\) is a ruled surface.

Example 64: MÃ¶bius band

Question. The MÃ¶bius band is a ruled surface with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}= {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) + v \mathbf{a}(u) \,, \quad u \in (0,2\pi), \, v \in \left( -\frac12, \frac12 \right) \,, \] where \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) = (\cos(u), \sin(u), 0) \] is the unit circle, and \[ \mathbf{a} = \left( -\sin \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \cos(u), -\sin \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \sin(u), \cos \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \right) \] is a vector which does a half rotation while going around the unit circle \({\pmb{\gamma}}\). In particular \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = \left( \left[ 1 - v \sin \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \right] \cos(u), \left[ 1 - v \sin \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \right] \sin(u), v \cos \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \right) \,. \]

  1. Compute the standard unit normal to \({\pmb{\sigma}}\).
  2. Prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is non orientable.

Solution.

  1. From the formula for \({\pmb{\sigma}}\), it is easy to compute that \[ {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\times {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}= \left( - \cos(u) \cos \left( \frac{u}{2} \right), - \sin(u) \cos \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) , - \sin \left( \frac{u}{2} \right) \right) \,. \] It is also immediate to check that \(\left\| {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\times {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v} \right\| = 1\), and therefore the principal unit normal of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \times {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}\,. \]

  2. Suppose by contradiction that \(\mathcal{S}\) is orientable. This means there exists a globally defined principal unit normal vector \[ \mathbf{N}\colon \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R}^3 \,. \] By definition of principal normal, we have either \[ \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}= \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \,. \] Consider the point \(\mathbf{p}= (1,0,0)\) on \(\mathcal{S}\). Notice that, by continuity, \(\mathbf{p}\) can be obtained via \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) through the limits \[ \mathbf{p}= \lim_{u \to 0^+} {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) = \lim_{u \to 2\pi^-} {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) \,. \] Since \(\mathbf{N}\) is continuous, the above implies \[ \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{p}) = \lim_{u \to 0^+} \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) = \lim_{u \to 2\pi^-} \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) \,. \tag{3.1}\] However, by direct calculation: \[\begin{align*} \lim_{u \to 0^+} \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) & = \lim_{u \to 0^+} \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} (u,0) = (-1,0,0) \\ \lim_{u \to 2\pi^-} \mathbf{N}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,0) & = \lim_{u \to 2\pi^-} \mathbf{N}_{{\pmb{\sigma}}} (u,0) = (1,0,0) \end{align*}\] This clearly contradicts (3.1). Therefore \(\mathbf{N}\) cannot exist, and \(\mathcal{S}\) is not orientable.

Example 65
Question. Show that the surface \[ S=\left\{(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{R}^{3} \, \colon \, x^{2}+ 10 xy + 16x^2 - z = 0\right\} \] is a ruled surface.

Solution. We shall make a change of variables. Notice that we can rearrange \[ \begin{aligned} x^{2}+10 x y+16 x^{2}-z & =0 \\ (x+8 y)(x+2 y) & = z . \end{aligned} \] Let \[ u=x+8 y, \qquad v=x+2 y \,. \] Then \(u v=z\) and \[ \begin{aligned} u-v & =6 y \quad \implies \quad y=\frac{u-v}{6} \\ x & =u-8 y \\ & =u-\frac{8(u-v)}{6} \\ & =\frac{4 v-u}{3} . \end{aligned} \] It follows that if \((x, y, z) \in S\) then \[ \begin{aligned} (x, y, z) & =\left(\frac{4 v-u}{3}, \frac{u-v}{6}, u v\right) \\ & =\left(-\frac{u}{3}, \frac{u}{6}, 0\right)+v\left(\frac{4}{3},-\frac{1}{6}, u\right) \\ & = {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) + v \mathbf{a}(u) \,, \end{aligned} \] where we have set \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\gamma}}(u) & = \left(-\frac{u}{3}, \frac{u}{6}, 0\right) \\ \mathbf{a}(u) & = \left(\frac{4}{3},-\frac{1}{6}, u\right) \,. \end{align*}\] Notice that \[ \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(u) = \left(-\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{6}, 0\right) \,. \] For \(u \neq 0\), we clearly have that \(\mathbf{a}(u)\) and \(\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(u)\) are linearly independent (the last component of \(\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(u)\) is \(0\)). For \(u = 0\) we have \[ \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(0) = \left(-\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{6}, 0\right) \,, \qquad \mathbf{a}(0) = \left(\frac{4}{3},-\frac{1}{6}, 0\right) \,, \] which are clearly linearly independent. Therefore, \(S\) is a ruled surface.

3.10 Surfaces of Revolution

Definition 66: Surface of revolution
Let \({\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (a,b) \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a smooth curve in the \((x,z)\)-plane, that is, \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(v) = (f(v), 0 , g(v)) \,. \] Suppose that \(f>0\). The surface \(\mathcal{S}\) obtained by rotating \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) about the \(z\)-axis is called surface of revolution. A chart for \(\mathcal{S}\) is given by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (\cos(u)f(v) , \sin(u) f(v), g(v))\,, \] with \(u \in (0,2\pi)\) and \(v \in (a,b)\).

Theorem 67
A surface of revolution is regular if and only if \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) is regular.

Example 68: Catenoid
Question. The catenary function is defined by \[ f(v) = \cosh (v) \,. \] The Catenoid \(\mathcal{S}\) is the surface of revolution obtained by rotating the catenary about the \(z\)-axis, that is, by rotating the curve \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(v) = \left( \cosh (v), 0 , v \right) \,. \] A chart \(\mathcal{S}\) is given by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (\cos(u) \cosh (v) , \sin(u) \cosh (v), v) \,, \] where \(u \in [0,2\pi)\) and \(v \in \mathbb{R}\). Prove that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a regular surface.

Solution. Note that \(f>0\) and \[ \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}= \left( \sinh (v), 0 , 1 \right) \,, \quad \left\| \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}} \right\|^2 = 1+ \sinh(v)^2 \geq 1 \,. \] Therefore \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) is regular. As \(\mathcal{S}\) is a surface of revolution, we conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) is regular.

3.11 First fundamental form

Definition 69: First fundamental form
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\). The first fundamental form of \(\mathcal{S}\) at \(\mathbf{p}\) is the bilinear symmetric map \[ I_{\mathbf{p}} \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\times T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R}\,, \quad I_{\mathbf{p}} (\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) := \mathbf{v}\cdot \mathbf{w}\,. \]

Definition 70: Coordinate functions on tangent plane
Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\). For each \(\mathbf{p}\in {\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\) we have \[ T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}= \operatorname{span} \{{\pmb{\sigma}}_u,{\pmb{\sigma}}_v\} \,, \] where \({\pmb{\sigma}}_u\) and \({\pmb{\sigma}}_v\) are evaluated at the point \((u,v) \in U\) such that \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v)=\mathbf{p}\,. \] Therefore, for each \(\mathbf{v}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\), there exist \(\lambda,\mu \in \mathbb{R}\) such that \[ \mathbf{v}= \lambda {\pmb{\sigma}}_u + \mu {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \,. \] The coordinate functions on \(T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) are the linear maps \[ du, dv \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R}\,, \quad du(\mathbf{v}) := \lambda \,, \quad dv(\mathbf{v}) := \mu \,. \]

Definition 71: First fundamental form of a chart
Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\). Define the functions \[ E , F , G \colon U \to \mathbb{R} \] by setting \[ E := {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \,, \quad F := {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \,, \quad G := {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \,. \] The first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is the quadratic form \[ \mathscr{F}_1 \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{R} \] defined by \[ \mathscr{F}_1 (\mathbf{v}) := E \, du^2(\mathbf{v}) + 2F \, du(\mathbf{v}) \, dv (\mathbf{v})+ G \, dv^2 (\mathbf{v}) \,, \] for all \(\mathbf{v}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\), and \(\mathbf{p}\in {\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\), where \(E,F,G\) are evaluated at \[ (u,v) = {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1}(\mathbf{p}) \,. \]

Theorem 72
Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\), and \(\mathbf{p}\in {\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\). Then \[ I_{\mathbf{p}} (\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) = (du (\mathbf{v}), dv(\mathbf{v}) ) \, \left( \begin{array}{cc} E & F \\ F & G \end{array} \right) \, (du(\mathbf{w}) , dv(\mathbf{w}))^T \,, \] for all \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\). In particular, \(\mathscr{F}_1\) is the quadratic form associated to the symmetric bilinear form \(I_{\mathbf{p}}\), that is, \[ \mathscr{F}_1 (\mathbf{v}) = I_{\mathbf{p}} (\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}) \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{v}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\,. \]

Definition 73
With a little abuse of notation, we also denote by \(\mathscr{F}_1\) the \(2 \times 2\) matrix \[ \mathscr{F}_1 := \left( \begin{array}{cc} E & F \\ F & G \end{array} \right) \,. \]

Proposition 74: First fundamental form and reparametrizations

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\) a regular chart. Suppose that \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon \widetilde{U} \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is a reparametrization of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\), that is, \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = {\pmb{\sigma}}\circ \Phi \,, \] where \(\Phi \colon \widetilde{U} \to U\) is a diffeomorphism. Denote the first fundamental forms of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) by, respectively, \[\begin{align*} \mathscr{F}_1 & = E \, du^2 + 2F \, du dv + G \, dv^2 \,, \\ \widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1 & = \widetilde{E} \, d\tilde{u}^2 + 2 \widetilde{F} \, d\tilde{u} d\tilde{v} + \widetilde{G} \, d\tilde{v}^2 \,. \end{align*}\]

  1. The matrices of \(\mathscr{F}_1\) and \(\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1\) are related by \[ \left( \begin{array}{cc} \widetilde{E} & \widetilde{F} \\ \widetilde{F} & \widetilde{G} \end{array} \right) = (J \Phi)^T \, \left( \begin{array}{cc} E & F \\ F & G \end{array} \right) \, J \Phi \,, \tag{3.2}\] where \(J\Phi\) is the Jacobian of \(\Phi\) \[ J\Phi = \left( \begin{array}{cc} \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial \tilde u} & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial \tilde v} \\ \dfrac{\partial v}{\partial \tilde u} & \dfrac{\partial v}{\partial \tilde v} \end{array} \right) \,. \]

  2. The linear maps \(du, dv\) and \(d\tilde{u}, d\tilde{v}\) are related by \[ \begin{aligned} du & = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tilde{u}} \, d\tilde{u} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tilde{v}} \, d\tilde{v} \\ dv & = \frac{\partial v}{\partial \tilde{u}} \, d\tilde{u} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial \tilde{v}} \, d\tilde{v} \end{aligned} \tag{3.3}\]

Example 75: FFF of Plane
Question. Let \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^3\), with \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) orthonormal. Consider the plane with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = \mathbf{a} + u \mathbf{p}+ v \mathbf{q} \,, \quad (u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \,. \] Prove that the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \]

Solution. We have \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = \mathbf{p}\,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = \mathbf{q} \,, \] and therefore, using that \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) are orthonormal, \[\begin{align*} E & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = \left\| \mathbf{p} \right\|^2 = 1 \\ F & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = \mathbf{p}\cdot \mathbf{q} = 0 \\ G & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = \left\| \mathbf{q} \right\|^2 = 1 \\ \end{align*}\] The first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is, therefore \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = E \, du^2 + 2 F\, du \, dv + G \, dv^2 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \]

Example 76: FFF of Plane in polar coordinates

Question. Let \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^3\), with \(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\) orthonormal. In polar coordinates, the plane is charted by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(\rho,\theta) = \mathbf{a} + \rho \cos(\theta) \mathbf{p}+ \rho \sin(\theta) \mathbf{q} \,, \quad \rho>0 , \, \theta \in (0,2\pi) \,. \]

  1. By direct calculation, show that the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = d\rho^2 + \rho^2 d\theta^2 \,. \]

  2. The first fundamental form of the plane in cartesian coordinates is \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \] Verify that \[ \left( \begin{array}{cc} E & F \\ F & G \end{array} \right) = (J \Phi)^T \, \left( \begin{array}{cc} \widetilde{E}& \widetilde{F}\\ \widetilde{F}& \widetilde{G} \end{array} \right) \, J \Phi \,, \] where \(\Phi\) is the change of variables from polar to cartesian coordinates.

Solution.

  1. Compute \(\mathscr{F}_1\) directly: \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\rho} & = \cos(\theta)\mathbf{p}+ \sin(\theta) \mathbf{q} \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} & = - \rho \sin(\theta) \mathbf{p}+ \rho \cos(\theta) \mathbf{q} \end{align*}\] and therefore \[\begin{align*} E & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\rho} \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\rho} \\ & = \cos^2(\theta)\left\| \mathbf{p} \right\|^2 + \sin^2(\theta)\left\| \mathbf{q} \right\|^2 + 2 \cos(\theta) \sin(\theta) \mathbf{p}\cdot \mathbf{q} \\ & = 1 \\ F & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\rho} \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} = 0 \\ G & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_{\theta} = r^2 \\ \end{align*}\] Then the first fundamental form is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = E \, d{\rho}^2 + 2 F\, d{\rho} \, d\theta + G \, d\theta^2 = d{\rho}^2 + {\rho}^2 d\theta^2 \,. \]

  2. The change of variables from polar to cartesian coordinates is \[ \Psi({\rho},\theta) = ( {\rho} \cos(\theta), {\rho} \sin (\theta) )\,. \] The Jacobian of \(\Psi\) is \[ J\Phi = \left( \begin{array}{cc} \cos(\theta) & - {\rho} \sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & {\rho} \cos(\theta) \end{array} \right) \,. \] The matrix of \({\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1\) is just the identity: \[ \left( \begin{array}{cc} \widetilde{E}& \widetilde{F}\\ \widetilde{F}& \widetilde{G} \end{array} \right) = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \,. \] Therefore, we have \[\begin{align*} (J \Phi)^T & \left( \begin{array}{cc} \widetilde{E}& \widetilde{F}\\ \widetilde{F}& \widetilde{G} \end{array} \right) \, J \Phi = (J \Phi)^T \, J \Phi \\ &= \left( \begin{array}{cc} \cos(\theta) & \sin(\theta) \\ -\rho \sin(\theta) & \rho \cos(\theta) \end{array} \right) \left( \begin{array}{cc} \cos(\theta) & - \rho \sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & \rho \cos(\theta) \end{array} \right) \\ &= \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \rho^2 \end{array} \right) = \left( \begin{array}{cc} E & F \\ F & G \end{array} \right) \,. \end{align*}\]

Example 77: FFF of Unit cylinder
Question. Consider the unit cylinder with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (\cos(u), \sin(u), v) \,, \quad (u,v) \in (0,2\pi) \times \mathbb{R}\,. \] Prove that the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \]

Solution. We have \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = (-\sin(u),\cos(u), 0 ) \,, \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = (0,0,1) \,, \] and therefore \[\begin{align*} E & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = 1 \\ F & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = 0 \\ G & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = 1 \\ \end{align*}\] Then the first fundamental form is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = E \, du^2 + 2 F\, du \, dv + G \, dv^2 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \]

Example 78

Solution. We compute \[ \begin{aligned} {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}& =(1,1,2 u) \\ {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}& =(-1,1,2 v) \\ E & = {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\cdot {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}= 2\left(1+2 u^{2}\right) \\ F & ={{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{u}\cdot {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}= 4 u v \\ G & = {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}\cdot {{\pmb{\sigma}}}_{v}= \left(1+2 v^{2}\right) \end{aligned} \] so that \[ \mathscr{F}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{cc} 2\left(1+2 u^{2}\right) & 4 u v \\ 4 u v & 2\left(1+2 v^{2}\right) \end{array}\right) \,. \]

3.12 Length of curves

Proposition 79

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface with chart \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathbb{R}^3\). Suppose \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}\colon (a,b) \to {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \subseteq \mathcal{S} \] is a smooth curve. Then \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}(u(t), v(t)) \,, \] for some smooth functions \(u,v \colon (a,b) \to \mathbb{R}\), and \[ \int_{a}^{b} \left\| \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t) \right\| \, dt = \int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{ E \dot{u}^2 + 2F \dot u \dot v + G \dot{v}^2 } \, dt \,, \] where in the above formula:

  • \(\dot u, \dot v\) are computed at \(t\),
  • \(E,F,G\) are computed at \((u(t),v(t))\).
Example 80: Curves on the Cone

Question. Consider the cone with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v)=(\cos(u) v, \sin (u) v, v) \,, \] where \(u \in (0,2\pi)\) and \(v >0\). Prove the following:

  1. The first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = 2 \, du^2 + u^2 \, dv^2 \,. \]

  2. Let \({\pmb{\gamma}}(t):= {\pmb{\sigma}}(t,t)\). The length of \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) is \[ \int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \left\| \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t) \right\| \, dt = \int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \sqrt{ t^2 + 2 } \, dt \,. \]

Solution.

  1. We have \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\sigma}}_u & = (- \sin(u)v, \cos(u)v,0) \\ {\pmb{\sigma}}_v & = (\cos(u), \sin (u), 1) \\ E & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = v^2 \\ F & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_u \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = 0 \\ G & = {\pmb{\sigma}}_v \cdot {\pmb{\sigma}}_v = 2 \end{align*}\] The first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = v^2 \, du^2 + 2 \, dv^2 \,. \]

  2. By definition we have \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}(t,t) \,, \] so that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}(u(t),v(t)) \] with \[ u(t) = t \,, \quad v(t) = t \,. \] In particular \[ \dot u = 1\,, \quad \dot v = 1 \] and \[\begin{align*} E(u(t),v(t)) & = E(t,t) = t^2 \\ F(u(t),v(t)) & = F(t,t) = 0 \\ G(u(t),v(t)) & = G(t,t) = 2 \,. \end{align*}\] Therefore, \[\begin{align*} \int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \left\| \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t) \right\| \, dt & = \int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \sqrt{ E \dot{u}^2 + 2F \dot u \dot v + G \dot{v}^2 } \, dt \\ & = \int_{\pi/2}^{\pi} \sqrt{ t^2 + 2 } \, dt \,. \end{align*}\]

3.13 Isometries

Definition 81: Local Isometry and Isometry

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \] be a smooth map. Denote the differential of \(f\) by \[ d_{\mathbf{p}}f \colon T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\to T_{f(\mathbf{p})} \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,. \]

We say that:

  1. \(f\) is a local isometry, if for all \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\) \[ \mathbf{v}\cdot \mathbf{w}= d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{v}) \cdot d_{\mathbf{p}}f (\mathbf{w}) \,, \quad \forall \, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\,. \tag{3.4}\] In this case, \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are said to be locally isometric.

  2. \(f\) is an isometry if:

    • \(f\) is a local isometry;
    • \(f\) is a diffeomorphism of \(\mathcal{S}\) into \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\).

    In this case, \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are said to be isometric.

Theorem 82
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \] a local isometry. Then \(f\) is a local diffeomorphims.

Theorem 83

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be a smooth map. They are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is a local isometry.
  2. Let \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) be a curve in \(\mathcal{S}\) and consider the curve \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}= f \circ {\pmb{\gamma}}\) on \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\). Then \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) have the same length.
Theorem 84

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be a local diffeomorphism. They are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is a local isometry.
  2. Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\) and consider the chart of \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) given by
    \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\,. \] Then \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) have the same first fundamental form, that is, \[ E = \widetilde{E} \,, \quad F = \widetilde{F} \,, \quad G = \widetilde{G} \,. \]
Theorem 85

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and consider charts \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,. \] Assume that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) have the same first fundamental form: \[ E = \widetilde{E} \,, \quad F = \widetilde{F} \,, \quad G = \widetilde{G} \,. \] We have:

  1. The surfaces \({\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are locally isometric.
  2. A local isometry is given by \[ f \colon {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \qquad f = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1} \,. \]
Example 86: Plane and Cylinder are locally isometric

Question. Consider the plane \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x = 0 \} \,, \] and the unit cylinder \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x^2 + y^2 = 1 \} \,. \] Define the function \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \qquad f(0,y,z) = (\cos(y),\sin(y),z) \,. \]

  1. Prove that \(f\) is smooth.
  2. Prove that \(f\) is a local isometry.

Note: This shows that the Plane and the Cylinder are locally isometric.

Solution.

  1. Note that \(f \in \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) because \[ \cos(y)^2 + \sin(y)^2 = 1\,, \] therefore \(f\) is well-defined. Moreover, \(f\) is the restriction to \(\mathcal{S}\) of the function \[ g \colon \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3 \,, \qquad g(x,y,z) = (\cos(y),\sin(y),z) \,. \] Since \(g\) is smooth, and \(g(\mathcal{S}) = \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\), by Theorem 37 we infer that \(g |_{\mathcal{S}} = f\) is smooth between \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\).

  2. Define the chart of \(\mathcal{S}\): \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) = (0,u,v) \,, \quad u,v \in \mathbb{R}\,. \] We already know that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is regular, with first fundamental form coefficients given by \[ E = 1 \,, \quad F = 1 \,, \quad G = 1 \,, \] and corresponding first fundamental form \[ {\mathscr{F}}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \] Define \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}= f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\). Therefore, \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}(u,v) = f(0,u,v) = (\cos(u),\sin(u),v) \,. \] We have that \[\begin{align*} {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{u}& = ( -\sin(u),\cos(u),0 ) \\ {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{v}& = (0,0,1) \\ \widetilde{E}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{u}\cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{u}= 1 \\ \widetilde{F}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{u}\cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{v}= 0 \\ \widetilde{G}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{v}\cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{v}= 1 \end{align*}\] Therefore, the first fundamental form of \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}= f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \] In particular, we have shown that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) have the same first fundamental form: \[ {\mathscr{F}}_1 = {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 \,. \] Since \(\mathcal{A} = \{{\pmb{\sigma}}\}\) is an atlas for \(\mathcal{S}\), by Theorem 85 we conclude that \(f\) is a local isometry of \(\mathcal{S}\) into \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\).

Example 87: Plane and Cone are locally isometric

Question. Consider the cone without tip \[ \mathcal{S}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x^2 + y^2 = z^2 \,, \,\, z > 0 \} \,, \] and the plane \[ \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}= \{ (x,y,z)\in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, z = 0 \} \,. \]

  1. Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be the chart of the Cone \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(\rho,\theta) = (\rho \cos(\theta), \rho \sin(\theta), \rho ) \,, \] where we define \[ U:= \left\{ (\rho,\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \, \colon \, \rho > 0 , \, \theta \in (0,2\pi) \right\} \,. \] Compute the first fundamental form \({\mathscr{F}}_1\) of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\).

  2. Let \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be the chart of the Plane \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}(\rho,\theta) = (a\rho \cos(b\theta), a\rho \sin(b\theta), 0 ) \] where \(a>0\) and \(b \in (0,1]\) are constants. Compute the first fundamental form \({\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1\) of \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\).

  3. Find coefficients \(a,b\) such that \[ {\mathscr{F}}_1 = {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 \,. \]

  4. Conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are locally isometric.

Solution.

  1. We have already computed in Example 81, that the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ {\mathscr{F}}_1 = 2 d\rho^2 + \rho^2 d\theta^2 \,. \]

  2. First of all, note that \[ \theta \in (0,2\pi), \quad b \in (0,1] \quad \implies \quad b \theta \in (0,2\pi) \,, \] showing that \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) is well defined for all \((\rho,\theta) \in U\). We compute \[\begin{align*} {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\rho} & = ( a \cos(b\theta), a \sin(b\theta), 0 ) \\ {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\theta} & = ( - ab \rho \sin(b \theta), ab\rho \cos(b\theta), 0 ) \\ \widetilde{E}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\rho} \cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\rho} = a^2 \\ \widetilde{F}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\rho} \cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\theta} = 0 \\ \widetilde{G}& = {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\theta} \cdot {\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}}_{\theta} = a^2b^2 \rho^2 \end{align*}\] from which we conclude that the first fundamental form of \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) is \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 = a^2 d\rho^2 + a^2b^2 \rho^2 d\rho^2 \,. \]

  3. Equating \({\mathscr{F}}_1\) and \({\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1\) we obtain \[ a^2 = 2 , \,\, a^2b^2 = 1 \quad \implies \quad a = \sqrt{2} \,, \,\, b = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \,. \] Note that \(a>0\) and \(0<b<1\), showing that \(a,b\) are admissible.

  4. For \(a = \sqrt{2}\) and \(b = 1/\sqrt{2}\) we have that \[ {\mathscr{F}}_1 = {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 \,. \] Since \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) are regular charts for \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\), respectively, from Theorem 84 we conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are locally isometric. Furthermore, the local isometry is given by \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \qquad f = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1} \,. \]

3.14 Angles between curves

Definition 88: Angle between curves
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface. Let \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) be curves on \(\mathcal{S}\) such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t_0) = \mathbf{p}= \widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}( t_0 ) \,. \] The angle \(\theta\) between \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) is \[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{ \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\cdot \dot{\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}}{ \| \dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\| \, \| \dot{\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}\| } \,, \] where \(\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) and \(\dot{\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}\) are evaluated at \(t_0\).

Theorem 89
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface, \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) a regular chart at \(\mathbf{p}\), and \({\pmb{\gamma}},\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) smooth curves on \(\mathcal{S}\) such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t_0) = \mathbf{p}= \widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}( t_0 ) \,. \] There exist smooth functions \(u,v,\tilde{u},\tilde{v}\) such that \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}(u(t),v(t)) \,, \quad \widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t) = {\pmb{\sigma}}(\tilde{u}(t),\tilde{v}(t)) \,. \] The angle between \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) is \[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{ E \dot u \dot{\tilde u} + F ( \dot u \dot{\tilde v}+ \dot{\tilde{u}} \dot v) + G \dot v \dot{\tilde v} }{ (E \dot{u}^2 + 2 F \dot u \dot v + G \dot{v}^2 )^{1/2} (E \dot{\tilde{u}}^2 + 2 F \dot{\tilde{u}} \dot{\tilde{v}} + G \dot{\tilde{v}}^2 )^{1/2} } \,, \] where \(E,F,G,\widetilde{E},\widetilde{F},\widetilde{G}\) are evaluated at \((u(t_0),v(t_0))\) and \(\dot{u},\dot{v}, \dot{\widetilde{u}}, \dot{\widetilde{v}}\) are evaluated at \(t_0\).

Example 90

Question. Let \(S\) be a surface with surface chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u, v) = \left(u, v, e^{u v}\right) \,. \]

  1. Calculate its first fundamental form.
  2. Calculate \(\cos (\theta)\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two curves \[\begin{align*} {\pmb{\gamma}}(t) & ={\pmb{\sigma}}(u(t), v(t)), \quad u(t)=t,\, v(t)=t \,, \\ \widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(t) & = {\pmb{\sigma}}( \tilde{u}(t),\tilde{v}(t) ) \,, \quad \tilde{u}(t) = 1 , \, \tilde{v}(t) = t \,. \end{align*}\]

Solution.

  1. We calculate \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}_u = \left(1,0, e^{u v} v\right), \quad {\pmb{\sigma}}_v=\left(0,1, e^{u v} u\right) \,. \] Therefore, the coefficients of the first fundamental form are \[\begin{align*} E(u,v) & =1+e^{2 u v} v^2 \\ F(u,v) & = e^{2 u v} u v \\ G(u,v) & = 1+e^{2 u v} u^2 \end{align*}\]

  2. The curves \({\pmb{\gamma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}\) intersect at \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}(1)=\widetilde{{\pmb{\gamma}}}(1)={\pmb{\sigma}}(1,1) \,. \] We calculate \[ \dot{u}(1)=1, \quad \dot{v}(1)=1, \quad \dot{\tilde{u}}(1)=0, \quad \dot{\tilde{v}}(1) = 1 \,, \] and \[\begin{align*} E(1,1) & = 1+e^2 \\ F(1,1) & =e^2 \\ G(1,1) & =1+e^2 \end{align*}\] Therefore \[ \begin{aligned} \cos \theta & =\frac{E \dot{u} \dot{\tilde{u}} + F \left(\dot{u} \dot{\tilde{v}}+ \dot{\tilde{u}} \dot{v}\right)+G \dot{v} \dot{\tilde{v}}}{\sqrt{E \dot{u}^2 + 2 F \dot{u} \dot{v} + G \dot{v}^2} \sqrt{E \dot{\tilde{u}}^2 + 2 F\dot{\tilde{u}} \dot{\tilde{v}}+ G \dot{\tilde{v}}^2}} \\ & =\frac{1+2 e^2}{\sqrt{2+4 e^2} \sqrt{1+e^2}}=\sqrt{\frac{1+2 e^2}{2+2 e^2}} \,. \end{aligned} \]

3.15 Conformal maps

Definition 91: Conformal map
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces, and \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \] a local diffeomorphism. We say that \(f\) is a conformal map if for all \(\mathbf{p}\in \mathcal{S}\) and \(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}\in T_{\mathbf{p}} \mathcal{S}\) is holds \[ \theta = \widetilde{\theta} \,, \] where:

  • \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\),
  • \(\widetilde{\theta}\) is the angle between \(d_{\mathbf{p}} f(\mathbf{v})\) and \(d_{\mathbf{p}} f(\mathbf{w})\).

In this case, we say that \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are conformal.

Proposition 92
Let \(f\) be a local isometry. Then \(f\) is a conformal map.

Theorem 93

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and \(f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be a local diffeomorphism. They are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is a conformal map.
  2. Let \({\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\) be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\) and consider the chart of \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) given by \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} \colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}} \,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}} = f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\,. \] Then, there exists \(\lambda \colon U \to \mathbb{R}\) such that \[ \widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1 = \lambda (u,v) \mathscr{F}_1 \,, \quad \forall \, (u,v) \in U \,, \] where \(\mathscr{F}_1\) and \(\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1\) are the first fundamental forms of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\), respectively.
Theorem 94

Let \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be regular surfaces and consider charts \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S}\,, \qquad \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\colon U \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,. \] Assume there exists \(\lambda \colon U \to \mathbb{R}\) such that \[ \widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1 = \lambda (u,v) \mathscr{F}_1 \,, \quad \forall \, (u,v) \in U \,, \] where \(\mathscr{F}_1\) and \(\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1\) are the first fundamental forms of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\), respectively. We have:

  1. The surfaces \({\pmb{\sigma}}(U)\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are conformal.
  2. A conformal map is given by \[ f \colon {\pmb{\sigma}}(U) \to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \qquad f = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\circ {\pmb{\sigma}}^{-1} \,. \]
Example 95: Stereographic Projection

Question. Denote the unit sphere by \[ \mathbb{S}^2 = \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1 \} \,, \] and consider the surface \[ \mathcal{S}= \mathbb{S}^2 \smallsetminus \{N\} \,, \] where the point \(N = (0,0,1)\) is the North Pole. Denote the plane \(\{z = 0\}\) by \[ \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}= \{ (x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \, \colon \, z = 0 \} \,, \] The plane \(\{z=0\}\) slices through the equator of the sphere. Let \(P=(x,y,z)\) be any point on \(\mathbb{S}^2\) except the north pole. The line joining the north pole to \(P\) intersects the plane \(\{z=0\}\) at the point \(P'\). The point \(P'\) defines the Stereographic Projection map, which is easily computed to be: \[ f \colon \mathcal{S}\to \widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\,, \quad f(x,y,z) = \left( \frac{x}{1-z},\frac{y}{1-z},0 \right) \,. \] Prove that:

  1. \(f\) is a conformal map.
  2. \(f\) is not a local isometry.

Note: In particular, the Sphere and the Plane are conformal.

Solution. It is not difficult to prove that \(f\) is invertible, with inverse given by \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) =\left( \frac{2u}{u^2+v^2+1}, \frac{2v}{u^2+v^2+1} , 1-\frac{2}{u^2+v^2+1}\right) \,. \] We have that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is a regular chart for \(\mathcal{S}\), forming an altas of one chart. It is straightforward to compute that the coefficients of the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) are \[ E = G = \lambda(u,v) := \frac{4}{(u^2+v^2+1)^2} \,, \qquad F = 0 \,. \] In particualar the fisrt fundamental form is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = \lambda(u,v) (du^2 + dv^2) \,. \] Define the chart of \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\): \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}= f \circ {\pmb{\sigma}}\,. \] Since \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is the inverse of \(f\), we have that \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}(u,v) = (u,v,0) \,. \] As already computed, the first fundamental form of \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\) is \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \] We can now conclude:

  1. We have that \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 = \frac{1}{\lambda} {\mathscr{F}}_1 \,. \] Since \(\mathcal{A} = \{{\pmb{\sigma}}\}\) is an atlas for \(\mathcal{S}\), by Theorem 93 we conclude that \(f\) is a conformal map.

  2. Since \({\lambda}\) is not always equal to \(1\), we have that \[ {\widetilde{\mathscr{F}}}_1 \neq {\mathscr{F}}_1 \,. \] Therefore, by Theorem 84, we conclude that \(f\) cannot be a local isometry.

Example 96: Sphere and Plane are conformal

Question. Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the plane \(\{z=0\}\) with chart \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}(u,v) := (u,v,0) \,, \quad u,v \in \mathbb{R}\,. \] Let \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) be the sphere \(\mathbb{S}^2\) with parametrization \[ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}(u,v) := \left( \cos(u) \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v), \sin(u) \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) , \tanh (v) \right) \,. \]

  1. Compute the first fundamental forms of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) and \(\widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}\).
  2. Show that \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are conformal.

Solution.

  1. As already computed, the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = du^2 + dv^2 \,. \] Using the identitities \[\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dv} \left( \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) \right) & = - \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) \tanh (v) \,, \\ \frac{d}{dv} \left( \tanh (v) \right) & = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2 (v) \,, \end{align*}\] we obtain \[\begin{align*} \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_u & = ( -\sin(u) \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) , \cos(u)\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) , 0 ) \\ \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_v & = ( -\cos(v) \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) \tanh(v) , -\sin(u) \mathop{\mathrm{sech}}(v) \tanh(v) , {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) ) \end{align*}\] By recalling that \[ {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2 (v) + {\tanh}^2 (v) = 1 \,, \] we compute \[\begin{align*} \widetilde{E} & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_u \cdot \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_u = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) (\cos^2(u) + \sin^2(u)) = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) \\ \widetilde{F} & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_u \cdot \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_v = 0 \\ \widetilde{G} & = \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_v \cdot \widetilde{{\pmb{\sigma}}}_v = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) ({\tanh}^2(v) + {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v)) = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) \\ \end{align*}\] Hence the first fundamental form of \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) is \[ \widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1 = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) \, \left( du^2 + dv^2 \right) \,. \]

  2. We have computed that \[ \widetilde{\mathscr{F}}_1 = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) \, \left( du^2 + dv^2 \right) = {\mathop{\mathrm{sech}}}^2(v) \,\mathscr{F}_1 \,. \] Since \(A = \{{\pmb{\sigma}}\}\) is an atlas for \(\mathcal{S}\), by Theorem 94 we conclude that \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\widetilde{\mathcal{S}}\) are conformal.

3.16 Conformal parametrizations

Definition 97: Conformal parametrization
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a regular surface and \[ {\pmb{\sigma}}\colon U \to \mathcal{S} \] be a regular chart of \(\mathcal{S}\). We say that \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) is a conformal parametrization if the first fundamental form of \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) satisfies \[ \mathscr{F}_1 = \lambda(u,v) ( du^2 + dv^2) \] for some function \(\lambda \colon U \to \mathbb{R}\).

Theorem 98
A conformal parametrization \({\pmb{\sigma}}\) preserves angles between vectors. Specifically, let \({\pmb{\gamma}}_{1}(t), {\pmb{\gamma}}_{2}(t)\) be curves in \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\) such that \({\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}_{1}\left(t_{0}\right), {\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}_{2}\left(t_{0}\right)\) make angle \(\theta\). If \[ {\pmb{\gamma}}_{3}(t)={\pmb{\sigma}}\left({\pmb{\gamma}}_{1}(t)\right), \qquad {\pmb{\gamma}}_{4}(t)={\pmb{\sigma}}\left({\pmb{\gamma}}_{2}(t)\right) \,, \] then \({\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}_{3}\left(t_{0}\right), {\dot{{\pmb{\gamma}}}}_{4}\left(t_{0}\right)\) also make angle \(\theta\).
